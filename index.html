Case Study — Quantitative NLP

Can Earnings Call Language Predict Stock Returns?

A perplexity-based NLP signal backtested across 3,400 earnings events, then applied to Micron Technology's historic AI-driven cycle.

Events 3,400

01 — The Question

What if how a CEO speaks matters more than what they report?

Earnings calls contain two layers of information. The first is financial — revenue, margins, guidance. Markets digest these in milliseconds. The second is linguistic — the hedge words, the confidence level, the structural complexity of how management frames uncertainty. This layer is slower, subtler, and largely unpriced.

Perplexity — a language model's measure of surprise at a given text — captures this second layer. High perplexity indicates evasive, uncertain, or linguistically complex communication. Low perplexity signals clarity and confidence. The hypothesis: perplexity inversely predicts post-earnings returns.

Core thesis
"When management speaks clearly, the stock goes up. When they hedge, it goes down. The signal is small but persistent — and it survives controls for earnings surprise, sentiment, and market conditions."
02 — Methodology

Building the signal

Simulated dataset with an embedded weak signal (~-5 bps/σ), designed to test whether standard quant techniques can detect and exploit linguistic patterns in earnings call text.

01
Feature Extraction

Four NLP features computed per transcript: Perplexity (language model surprise), Sentiment (Loughran-McDonald), Readability (Fog Index proxy), and Uncertainty keywords (hedging word density).

02
Portfolio Construction

Each quarter, rank stocks by perplexity z-score into quintiles. Go long Q1 (low perplexity = clear communication) and short Q5 (high perplexity = evasive language). Measure returns at 1, 5, and 30-day horizons.

03
Robustness Suite

Test across horizons, vol regimes, market cap buckets. Run kitchen-sink regressions with controls for earnings surprise, revenue growth, and market beta. Newey-West standard errors for autocorrelation. VIF diagnostics for collinearity.

Across 20 quarters, the long/short perplexity spread compounds to +66.7% with a Sharpe of 4.72 and a 100% quarterly win rate.

Key finding
"The signal breaks in large caps at the 30-day horizon. Efficient price discovery in mega-cap names absorbs the linguistic information before the monthly window closes."
R²
Feature Contribution Ranking

Perplexity dominates at 4.10% marginal R², followed by Sentiment at 1.85%. Readability and uncertainty keywords contribute -3 and -4 bps/σ respectively but are ~55% redundant with perplexity.

Sentiment adds orthogonal information — the only feature that is genuinely independent.

NW
Newey-West Adjustment

Newey-West standard errors confirm low autocorrelation in the signal. The t-statistics barely change after adjustment, indicating the returns are not serially correlated — each earnings event is largely independent.

Acceleration FQ3'24 — FQ4'25 · $11.7B rev · 42.2% GM

Formulaic superlatives: "record revenue," "strong execution," "exceeded the high end." Decreasing vocabulary diversity as narrative narrows to pricing power, supply tightness, AI demand. Predicted perplexity: Q1-Q2 (low, stable). Strongest forward return signal.

Peak Confidence + Divergence FQ2'26 · $23.9B rev · 74.4% GM

Surface: "Exceptional results," "strategic asset," 30% dividend hike, first-ever 5-year customer agreement. Maximally confident prepared remarks.

Subsurface: CFO refused FQ4 gross margin guidance. CapEx raised to >$25B with no ceiling. Avoided long-term bit growth specifics. Admitted diminishing marginal pricing impact at 81% margins. Predicted: Q1 prepared remarks, Q3 in Q&A. The divergence is the signal.

06 — Thesis + Limitations

Net signal: bullish but weakening

At the current linguistic profile, MU sits in Q1/Q2 — the quintile associated with the strongest forward returns. Sustained low perplexity across multiple quarters, reinforced by dividend hikes and strategic agreements, creates a compounding positive signal.

However, three factors temper the outlook: the prepared-vs-Q&A divergence suggests the next quarter's language may shift; MU's $362B market cap places it where the 30-day signal loses statistical significance; and the CapEx escalation pattern resembles the early stages of what historically precedes cycle-peak admissions.

Key monitoring variable
"Does Mehrotra provide FQ4 gross margin guidance on the June FQ3'26 call? If yes, the divergence closes — bullish. If he again declines, the hedging pattern strengthens and the signal weakens."

Limitations

Simulated data
The backtest uses a synthetic dataset with an embedded -5 bps/σ signal. Applying it to actual transcripts requires acknowledging that individual-stock NLP signals are noisier than cross-sectional portfolio signals.
Large-cap degradation
The signal loses significance in large caps at 30D. MU at $362B falls squarely in this cohort. The 5D horizon remains actionable, but monthly holding periods are unreliable.
N=1 application
Analyzing a single company's linguistic arc, however detailed, does not constitute a statistical test. The framework is designed for cross-sectional portfolios, not individual stock conviction.
Proxy, not literal
"Perplexity" is used as a linguistic complexity measure, not a literal GPT perplexity score on the transcript. A production system would require running a language model on the actual text.
